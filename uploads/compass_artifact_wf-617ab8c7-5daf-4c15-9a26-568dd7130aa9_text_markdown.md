# AI Automation Timing Predictions by Job Category: A Catalogue of Major Studies

## TL;DR
- The studies that make genuinely *specific, dated* automation predictions cluster around **2030** (WEF, McKinsey, Forrester, Goldman Sachs) and **2027–2031** (METR's capability-extrapolation work); almost none claim a named occupation will be "fully replaced" by a date — they forecast share-of-tasks automated, net job change, or capability thresholds.
- For your priority categories: **graphic design** is now flagged by WEF as a declining job for the first time (driven by GenAI); **advertising/agency work** has the single most specific role-level forecast (Forrester: 7.5% of US agency jobs / 32,000 lost to automation by 2030); **coding/web development** has the most aggressive capability timeline (METR: AI able to do month-long software tasks at 50% reliability with a central estimate of mid-2029, and "half the probability in 2027" on the faster recent trend).
- The most rigorous, peer-reviewed work (OpenAI/Wharton's "GPTs are GPTs" in *Science*; Acemoglu in *Economic Policy*) deliberately **AVOIDS** timing predictions — the specific dates come mostly from consultancies (McKinsey, Goldman, Forrester, PwC) and think tanks (WEF), whose methods are surveys and economic modelling, not peer review.

## Key Findings

The research landscape splits into four methodological camps, and which camp a study belongs to largely determines how specific its timing is:

1. **Employer-survey forecasts (WEF, Forrester, PwC)** — give the most concrete dates and role-level numbers because they simply ask employers what they plan to do by a fixed year (usually 2030).
2. **Economic/task-exposure modelling (McKinsey, Goldman Sachs, Acemoglu)** — give dated share-of-hours-automated figures (e.g., ~30% of US work hours by 2030) but explicitly decline to predict job losses per occupation.
3. **ML capability extrapolation (METR)** — gives dated *capability* thresholds (when AI can complete tasks of a given length) rather than labour-market outcomes.
4. **Observed-usage and ex-post empirical work (Anthropic Economic Index, Stanford Digital Economy Lab, Brookings)** — measures what is happening now rather than predicting dates; valuable as a reality check.

A critical caveat threads through all of this: the academically peer-reviewed studies (OpenAI's *Science* paper, Acemoglu) are the ones that most explicitly refuse to make timing predictions, while the most specific dated predictions come from non-peer-reviewed institutional reports.

## Details

### World Economic Forum — Future of Jobs Report 2025
- **Authors/Publisher:** World Economic Forum (Insight Report); Centre for the New Economy and Society, headed by Till Leopold (Head of Work, Wages and Job Creation); ISBN 978-2-940631-90-2.
- **Date:** January 2025 (released at Davos, 7–8 January 2025).
- **URL:** https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/ — full PDF: https://reports.weforum.org/docs/WEF_Future_of_Jobs_Report_2025.pdf
- **Methodology:** Survey of over 1,000 employers representing more than 14 million workers across 22 industry clusters and 55 economies (Future of Jobs Survey 2024); forecasts are employer-reported expectations for the 2025–2030 window.
- **Specific timing predictions (all by 2030):**
  - Net structural churn of 22% of jobs: 170 million created (14% of today's employment), 92 million displaced (8%), net +78 million (+7%).
  - AI and information processing technology: +11 million jobs created, −9 million displaced (more job churn than any other technology trend).
  - Robots and autonomous systems: net −5 million jobs (largest net displacer).
  - Broadening digital access: +19 million / −9 million.
  - **Graphic designers** appear on the declining lists for the first time — ranked roughly #11 fastest-declining and #13 largest-declining. Per the WEF press release (Till Leopold), cashiers and administrative assistants "remain among the fastest declining but are now joined by roles including graphic designers as generative AI rapidly reshapes the labour market." This is a reversal from the 2023 edition, where graphic design was "moderately growing."
  - Fastest-growing roles (% terms): Big Data Specialists, FinTech Engineers, AI & Machine Learning Specialists, Software & Application Developers, Security Management Specialists; UI/UX Designers in the top 15.
  - Other steepest declines: cashiers/ticket clerks, administrative assistants/executive secretaries, data entry clerks, accountants/auditors, postal clerks, bank tellers, printing workers.
  - Task split: in 2025, 47% of tasks done mainly by humans, 22% mainly by technology, 30% by a combination; by 2030 employers expect these to be "nearly evenly split" (~33% each). Of the ~15-point reduction in human-only tasks, ~82% is attributed to automation and ~19% to expanded human-machine collaboration.
- **Caveat:** Exact per-role net numbers exist only inside chart images (Figures 2.2–2.5), not in the report's running text. The per-role ranking positions for graphic design (#11/#13) come from secondary readings of those charts.
- **Predecessors:** The Future of Jobs Report 2023 (May 2023) projected 83 million jobs displaced and 69 million created by 2027 (net −14 million); the 2020 edition projected 85 million jobs displaced by 2025.

### Goldman Sachs Research — "The Potentially Large Effects of Artificial Intelligence on Economic Growth" (March 2023) and follow-ups
- **Authors:** Joseph Briggs and Devesh Kodnani (Global Economics), with later labour-market notes by Sarah Dong and Elsie Peng.
- **Date:** 26 March 2023 (original); follow-up labour-market notes March 2025 and 2026.
- **URL:** https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/generative-ai-could-raise-global-gdp-by-7-percent and https://www.goldmansachs.com/insights/articles/how-will-ai-affect-the-us-labor-market
- **Methodology:** Analysis of databases detailing task content of 900+ occupations; combined task-level exposure scores with economic modelling.
- **Specific predictions:**
  - Headline: generative AI could expose the equivalent of 300 million full-time jobs globally to automation; roughly two-thirds of US occupations exposed to some degree, and of those, a quarter to half of the workload could be replaced.
  - In the US, AI could automate tasks accounting for 25% of all work hours.
  - By sector (2023 report): administrative (46% of tasks exposed), legal (44%), arts/design/media/entertainment (26%), construction (6%), maintenance (4%).
  - Timeline (Briggs base case): wide-scale adoption over ~10 years, 6–7% of workers displaced during the transition (range 3–14%), ~0.6pp rise in unemployment; productivity/GDP gains begin around 2027 and run through the 2030s.
  - 2025/2026 updates: AI's measurable macroeconomic impact in the US expected from 2027; if current use cases expanded economy-wide, ~2.5% of US employment would be at risk; a 2026 note (Elsie Peng) estimated AI was cutting roughly 16,000 US jobs a month, hitting entry-level/Gen Z workers hardest, using a substitution-vs-augmentation framework built on the IMF complementarity index.
- **Caveat:** Goldman's own later data found aggregate labour-market impacts "still negligible" as of early 2025; the firm's legal-exposure estimate fell from "44% of tasks" (2023) to an implied ~17% of legal jobs at risk (2025) — a useful illustration of how volatile these estimates are.

### McKinsey Global Institute — "Generative AI and the Future of Work in America" (2023) and successors
- **Authors/Publisher:** McKinsey Global Institute (Kweilin Ellingrud, Saurabh Sanghvi, and colleagues).
- **Dates:** July 2023 ("Generative AI and the future of work in America"); May 2024 ("A new future of work: the race to deploy AI…"); 2025 ("agents, robots, and us" skill-partnerships report).
- **URL:** https://www.mckinsey.com/mgi/our-research/generative-ai-and-the-future-of-work-in-america and https://www.mckinsey.com/mgi/our-research/a-new-future-of-work-the-race-to-deploy-ai-and-raise-skills-in-europe-and-beyond
- **Methodology:** Task/activity-based automation modelling (building on O*NET work-activity data), adoption-scenario modelling ("early"/"late"), productivity estimation.
- **Specific timing predictions (by 2030):**
  - Verbatim: "Without generative AI, our research estimated, automation could take over tasks accounting for 21.5 percent of the hours worked in the US economy by 2030. With it, that share has now jumped to 29.5 percent" — i.e., up to ~30% of US work hours automatable by 2030 (midpoint of a wide adoption range); ~27% in Europe.
  - 12 million additional occupational transitions in the US by 2030; lower-wage workers up to 14× more likely to need to change occupations.
  - Job categories that continue to decline: office support, customer service, food service. STEM, creative, and business/legal professions enhanced rather than eliminated. Automated-hours jump (with GenAI acceleration) is largest for STEM (+16pts to ~30%), creatives/arts management (+15pts to ~25%), and business/legal.
  - 2025 report: currently demonstrated technologies could in theory automate ~57% of US work hours (agents 44%, robots 13%); midpoint adoption scenario yields ~$2.9 trillion/year of US economic value by 2030.
- **Caveat:** McKinsey explicitly states "our research does not lead us to estimate job losses"; figures are about hours/tasks and adoption scenarios, not headcount.

### Frey & Osborne (2013/2017) — the original timed prediction (and its critics)
- **Authors:** Carl Benedikt Frey and Michael A. Osborne, Oxford Martin School / Oxford University Engineering Science.
- **Date:** September 2013 working paper; published 2017 in *Technological Forecasting and Social Change*.
- **URL:** https://oms-www.files.svdcdn.com/production/downloads/academic/The_Future_of_Employment.pdf
- **Methodology:** A Gaussian process classifier (machine-learning) trained on 70 hand-labelled occupations, extrapolated across 702 O*NET occupations using 9 bottleneck-skill variables; output is a "probability of computerisation."
- **Specific timing prediction:** ~47% of total US employment at "high risk" of automation "over the next decade or two" (i.e., roughly by the early-to-mid 2030s). Telemarketers, title examiners, and hand sewers scored ~99% probability.
- **Status/critique:** The most-cited timed automation study ever, but heavily criticised. Coelli & Borland (Melbourne Institute, 2019, SSRN 3472764) found the predictions did not explain actual 2013–2018 US occupational employment changes; ITIF (2022) found only a modest 0.26 correlation between computerisation-risk scores and actual job loss, and noted insurance underwriters (high risk) grew 16.4% while recreational therapists (low risk) declined 8.9%. Treat the 47% figure as a cautionary tale about extrapolation, not a validated forecast.

### OpenAI / University of Pennsylvania — "GPTs are GPTs"
- **Authors:** Tyna Eloundou, Sam Manning, Pamela Mishkin (OpenAI), Daniel Rock (Wharton, University of Pennsylvania).
- **Date:** March 2023 (arXiv working paper); peer-reviewed version in *Science* vol. 384, pp. 1306–1308 (2024), DOI 10.1126/science.adj0998.
- **URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.10130 and https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adj0998
- **Methodology:** A new exposure rubric applied to O*NET tasks via both human annotators and GPT-4 as classifier; "exposure" defined as potential for LLMs to reduce task time by ≥50% at equal quality.
- **Findings (NOT dated):** ~80% of the US workforce could have at least 10% of tasks affected by LLMs; ~19% could see at least 50% of tasks affected. With LLM-powered software, 47–56% of all tasks become exposed. Higher-income jobs face greater exposure.
- **Crucial caveat (verbatim):** "We do not make predictions about the development or adoption timeline of such LLMs." This is the key peer-reviewed exposure study, and it deliberately gives NO timing.

### METR — "Measuring AI Ability to Complete Long Tasks" (capability extrapolation)
- **Authors:** Thomas Kwa, Ben West et al., Model Evaluation & Threat Research (METR).
- **Date:** 19 March 2025 (arXiv 2503.14499 and METR blog).
- **URL:** https://metr.org/blog/2025-03-19-measuring-ai-ability-to-complete-long-tasks/ and https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14499
- **Methodology:** Defined a "50%-task-completion time horizon" — the human-task-duration at which a model succeeds 50% of the time — measured across 170+ software/research tasks (HCAST, RE-Bench, SWAA) for 12 models from 2019–2025, using logistic regression inspired by item-response theory.
- **Specific timing predictions (capability, software domain):**
  - The 50% time horizon has "doubled approximately every seven months since 2019" (possibly every ~4 months in 2024–2025). For reference, GPT-2 ≈ 2 seconds; Claude 3.7 Sonnet ≈ 50 minutes; o3 ≈ 110 minutes.
  - Extrapolation: AI able to complete one-month-long (167-work-hour) software tasks at 50% reliability arrives with an 80% confidence interval of mid-2028 to mid-2030 (**central estimate ~mid/late 2029**). Per the paper, "if future progress instead follows the 2024–2025 trend, 1-month AI would arrive sooner, with half the probability in 2027 and even late 2026."
  - Stated implication: "within 5 years, AI systems will be capable of automating many software tasks that currently take humans a month."
- **Caveat:** This is a capability forecast, not a labour-displacement forecast; the domain is exclusively software/research tasks; the authors stress measurement caveats and external-validity limits. Independent statistical reviews argue the ~11-point dataset is too sparse for confident long-horizon extrapolation.

### Forrester — "Agency AI-Powered Workforce Forecast, 2030 (US)" (most role-specific marketing forecast)
- **Authors:** Jay Pattisall and Michael O'Grady, Forrester Research.
- **Date:** June 2023 (released around Cannes Lions).
- **URL:** https://www.forrester.com/press-newsroom/forrester-agency-ai-workforce-2030/
- **Methodology:** Workforce modelling of US advertising-agency employment combining automation potential, ML, and generative-AI influence scores by role.
- **Specific timing predictions (by 2030):**
  - Verbatim: "by 2030, US advertising agencies and related services companies will lose 32,000 jobs to automation — 7.5% of the total agency workforce" (2022–2030).
  - "By 2030, generative AI will account for nearly a third of automated advertising jobs."
  - Job losses concentrated in clerical/secretarial/administrative (28% of losses), sales (22%), market research (18%).
  - Growing share: management, PR, creative roles, market research, software (incl. web and digital interface designers), data science. Globally, digital marketing and strategy specialists' headcount to grow >20% by 2028.
  - Key principle: "originality is the most significant factor that lowers a job's automation potential" — editors, writers, and authors are among the *least* automatable creative roles; the report predicts a "workforce inversion" away from cheap junior talent toward senior creators paired with AI assistants.
- **Caveat:** A vendor research report (full text paywalled). Near-term (next-two-year) job loss expected to be "modest" pending IP/copyright/bias resolution. Sir Martin Sorrell's widely quoted remark that "there are 250,000 people in media planning and buying" potentially exposed is an executive comment, not a Forrester model output.

### PwC — Global AI Jobs Barometer (2025 and 2026)
- **Publisher:** PwC (Global); Global Chief AI Officer Joe Atkinson.
- **Date:** June 2025 (2025 edition); 2026 edition released subsequently.
- **URL:** https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/services/ai/ai-jobs-barometer.html
- **Methodology:** Analysis of close to a billion job ads across six continents plus thousands of company financial reports; uses the AI Occupational Exposure index and IMF complementarity methodology.
- **Findings (trend-based, not dated by occupation):** Productivity growth nearly quadrupled in the most AI-exposed industries (7%→27%, 2018–2024); AI-skilled workers command a 56% wage premium (up from 25% the prior year); jobs requiring AI skills grew 7.5% even as total postings fell 11.3%; skills churn 66% faster in the most exposed jobs. The 2026 edition reports productivity growth 40% higher at the most AI-exposed firms.
- **Stance:** Notably optimistic — "jobs are growing in virtually every type of AI-exposed occupation, including highly automatable ones" — and a useful counterweight to displacement narratives. It does not give per-occupation displacement dates.

### Anthropic Economic Index (observed usage, not prediction)
- **Publisher:** Anthropic (Societal Impacts team).
- **Dates:** First report February 2025; subsequent reports through June 2026.
- **URL:** https://www.anthropic.com/economic-index and https://www.anthropic.com/news/the-anthropic-economic-index
- **Methodology:** Privacy-preserving classification (Clio) of millions of anonymised Claude.ai and API conversations mapped to ~17,000 O*NET tasks.
- **Findings:** Coding/Computer & Mathematical tasks dominate (35% of Claude.ai conversations, 44% of API traffic); ~36% of occupations see AI use in at least a quarter of their tasks; ~4% across three-quarters of tasks. A later report found roughly 49% of jobs had at least 25% of their tasks performed using Claude. Usage skews to augmentation over automation. Explicitly present-tense: "We don't … attempt to forecast the future."

### Stanford Digital Economy Lab — "Canaries in the Coal Mine?" (ex-post empirical)
- **Authors:** Erik Brynjolfsson, Bharat Chandar, Ruyu Chen.
- **Date:** August 2025 (last revised November 2025).
- **URL:** https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/publication/canaries-in-the-coal-mine-six-facts-about-the-recent-employment-effects-of-artificial-intelligence/
- **Methodology:** High-frequency administrative payroll data from ADP (largest US payroll provider).
- **Findings (verbatim):** "since the widespread adoption of generative AI, early-career workers (ages 22-25) in the most AI-exposed occupations have experienced a 13 percent relative decline in employment even after controlling for firm-level shocks" — concentrated in software development, customer service, and clerical work, and concentrated where AI automates rather than augments. Older/less-exposed workers held steady or grew. The authors note the effect becomes statistically significant only in 2024 once the broadest controls are applied. Not a dated forecast, but the strongest real-data evidence that displacement is already beginning in coding and customer-service entry-level roles.

### Brookings Institution — "Generative AI, the American Worker, and the Future of Work"
- **Authors:** Molly Kinder, Xavier de Souza Briggs, Mark Muro, Sifan Liu (Brookings Metro).
- **Date:** 12 April 2024 (with 2025 follow-ups, including an NBER-linked adaptive-capacity analysis).
- **URL:** https://www.brookings.edu/articles/generative-ai-the-american-worker-and-the-future-of-work/
- **Methodology:** Analysis of OpenAI task-exposure data (ChatGPT-4) across 1,000+ occupations.
- **Findings (not dated):** More than 30% of workers could see at least 50% of their tasks affected by ChatGPT-4; 85% could see at least 10% affected. Higher-paid, higher-education white-collar work (coders, writers, analysts, lawyers, engineers, marketers) is the most exposed — a reversal of prior automation patterns. No per-occupation date given.

### Daron Acemoglu (MIT) — "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI"
- **Author:** Daron Acemoglu (MIT; 2024 Nobel laureate in economic sciences).
- **Date:** May 2024 (NBER WP 32487); published 2025 in *Economic Policy* vol. 40(121), pp. 13–58.
- **URL:** https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487 and https://shapingwork.mit.edu/research/the-simple-macroeconomics-of-ai/
- **Methodology:** Task-based macro modelling applying a version of Hulten's theorem to existing task-exposure and productivity estimates.
- **Findings:** Only ~5% of tasks will be profitably performed by AI within ~10 years (of ~20% technically exposed); total factor productivity gain no more than ~0.66% over 10 years (likely <0.53%); GDP boost ~1–1.8%. A deliberate, peer-reviewed counter to Goldman/McKinsey optimism. The dated element is the 10-year horizon for the ~5%-of-tasks figure.

### Supporting datapoint — Graphite on AI-written content
- **Publisher:** Graphite (vertical AI growth agency), with data cited in the WEF reports.
- **URL:** https://graphite.io/five-percent/more-articles-are-now-created-by-ai-than-humans and updated https://graphite.io/five-percent/ai-now-writes-as-many-online-articles-as-humans-do
- **Finding:** Using a Common Crawl sample (~43k–65k English articles) and AI-detection tools, Graphite found the quantity of AI-generated articles surpassed human-written ones around November 2024, rising from 2.2% in January 2020; the share has since plateaued near 50% (49.6% in Q1 2025, 50.9% in Q4 2025, 49.9% in Q1 2026). This is the most concrete "content creation" datapoint, but it is a vendor study measuring publication volume, not employment.

## Recommendations

**If you need the most defensible, dated, role-level predictions for your priority categories, use these in this order:**

1. **Marketing/advertising/agency work →** Forrester's *Agency AI-Powered Workforce Forecast, 2030* is the only study with a hard role-level number (7.5% / 32,000 US agency jobs by 2030, with the clerical 28% / sales 22% / market-research 18% breakdown). Pair it with WEF 2025 for the broader "graphic designers now declining" signal and Goldman's "26% of arts/design/media tasks exposed."
2. **Web development/coding →** Use METR's time-horizon paper for the capability timeline (month-long software tasks at 50% reliability, central estimate ~2029, half-probability in 2027 on the faster trend) and Stanford's "Canaries" paper for evidence that entry-level software employment is *already* down ~13%. Note WEF still lists software developers among the fastest-*growing* roles — a genuine tension worth flagging in any write-up.
3. **Content creation/copywriting →** There is no peer-reviewed dated forecast. The strongest concrete datapoint is Graphite's finding that AI-generated articles surpassed human-written ones around November 2024 and plateaued near 50% of sampled web articles. Forrester counsels that writers/editors are among the *least* automatable creative roles. Treat "content production replaced by 2030" claims as unsupported by rigorous research.
4. **Data analysis →** Goldman (administrative/analytical tasks highly exposed), Anthropic (data-entry keyers and database tasks show high Claude coverage), and Brookings (analysts highly exposed) — but none give a clean per-occupation date.
5. **Graphic design/video production →** WEF 2025 (graphic design newly declining, ~#11) and Goldman's 26%-of-tasks figure for arts/design/media. No study gives a video-production-specific date.

**Benchmarks that should change your conclusions:** If METR's doubling time stays at ~7 months, the ~2029 central estimate holds; if it accelerates to ~4 months (the 2024–2025 trend), pull software-automation timelines forward to 2026–2027. If Stanford's entry-level decline spreads to mid-career cohorts in 2026–2027 payroll data, displacement is running ahead of the consultancy forecasts. If Goldman's "negligible aggregate impact" finding flips to measurable unemployment effects around its predicted 2027 inflection, that validates the modelling camp over the optimistic PwC camp.

## Caveats
- **Specificity is inversely correlated with rigour.** The most precise dated predictions come from non-peer-reviewed consultancy/think-tank reports (WEF, Forrester, McKinsey, Goldman). The peer-reviewed studies (OpenAI in *Science*, Acemoglu in *Economic Policy*) explicitly refuse to predict timing.
- **"Exposure" ≠ "automation" ≠ "job loss."** Every serious study stresses this distinction. Exposure measures technical potential; actual displacement depends on adoption cost, regulation, and economics. McKinsey, OpenAI, Goldman, and Anthropic all caveat that exposed tasks may be augmented rather than replaced.
- **The Frey-Osborne precedent is a warning.** The most famous dated prediction (47% by ~the 2030s) has largely failed to verify against actual employment data. Apply similar skepticism to current 2030 forecasts.
- **No reputable study endorses claims like "web development 60% automated by 2028" or "content production replaced by 2030."** Those specific framings appear in marketing/SEO blog content, not in institutional research. The closest legitimate analogues are METR's capability dates and Forrester's 7.5% agency figure.
- **Watch for circularity in 2025–2026 sources.** Several AI-impact figures now circulate through AI-written aggregator articles (and Graphite's own data shows ~50% of web articles are AI-generated), so verify any striking statistic against the primary institutional source linked above.
