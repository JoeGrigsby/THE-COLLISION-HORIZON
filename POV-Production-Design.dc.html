▸ PERSPECTIVE · PRODUCTION DESIGN

When Making the Work Stops Being the Work

The repetitive craft of production design is the first thing connected creative-automation systems absorb. A view on when the role declines — and what it becomes instead.

JG
Joe Grigsby
Working group contributor · July 2026
in LINKEDIN ↗

For decades, agencies have relied on production designers to turn creative ideas into the hundreds, sometimes thousands, of assets needed to run a modern campaign.

They resize layouts. Build channel adaptations. Update prices and product information. Create language versions. Swap imagery. Prepare files. Produce social, display, ecommerce and retail variations.

It is skilled and important work. But a lot of it is also rules-based, repetitive and increasingly easy for machines to handle.

The technology needed to automate this work is no longer theoretical. Nearly every major player in the advertising ecosystem is now building, buying or promoting some version of it.

WPP has Open Pro, Composable Content Engine and WPP Production. Publicis has CoreAI. Omnicom has Omni. Dentsu is combining its own intelligence with Adobe GenStudio. Havas has introduced Converged.AI and Vermeer. Brandtech has Pencil. Adobe, Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, Smartly and Celtra are building automated creative generation, adaptation, publishing and optimization directly into the platforms where campaigns are created and distributed.

And these systems are starting to connect.

In June 2026, WPP announced that it would become the first advertising partner to pilot Meta's newest creative solution, integrated directly into WPP Open. The system is designed to analyze campaign performance, identify what is working, recommend improvements, generate new concepts and validate them through testing.

That matters because it begins to close the loop between media performance and creative production.

The system does not simply help someone make an ad faster. It can understand what is performing, recommend what should be made next and help generate the new work, all without creating a separate workflow.

That is a much bigger change.

The question is no longer whether production design will be automated. It is when the economics become compelling enough for agencies and clients to rebuild their operating models around it.

The likely inflection point: 2027 to 2028

My expectation is that agencies will begin to see a meaningful decline in dedicated production-design roles during 2027 and 2028, with the shift becoming structural by 2030.

The first phase is already underway. Agencies are using AI to generate copy and imagery, create social variations, resize assets, remove backgrounds, localize content and produce basic video executions.

For now, a lot of this still feels experimental. In many organizations, the new tools sit next to the traditional production process rather than replacing it.

The next phase will look different.

The tools will be connected to brand guidelines, approved assets, product information, media plans, workflow systems, performance data and publishing platforms. Instead of asking a designer to produce 100 variations, a marketer or creative lead will approve a system that generates them automatically within a defined set of rules.

At that point, automation will not simply help production designers do more work. It will reduce the number of production designers needed to do it.

By 2030, I would expect many large agencies to employ 30% to 50% fewer people whose primary responsibility is manual asset adaptation and versioning. The decline could be even greater in highly standardized areas such as paid social, display, ecommerce, CRM and retail media.

That is my directional estimate, not a published industry forecast. The more certain prediction is that agencies will employ fewer people devoted exclusively to execution and more people responsible for setting up, supervising and improving automated production systems.

The role will split, not disappear

Production design will not go away completely. But the work is likely to split into three very different categories.

At one end will be highly automated production. This includes resizing, formatting, versioning, localization, simple animation, product-image adaptation and channel compliance. Machines will perform most of this work, while people handle exceptions and review the output.

At the other end will be high-craft production that requires judgment, taste, complex retouching, visual storytelling, unusual formats or close collaboration with creative directors. This work will remain human-led, even as AI makes it faster.

In the middle, a new group of roles will emerge: creative-automation operators, brand-system designers, content engineers, synthetic-production leads and creative quality specialists. These people will not manually construct every asset. They will build the templates, rules, prompts, data connections and approval systems through which thousands of assets are created.

The future role is less about making every execution and more about building and governing the system that makes the executions.

The implications for agencies

The immediate benefit is easy to see. Campaigns can move faster, cost less and include far more creative variation. Smaller brands will gain access to production capabilities that were previously available only to large advertisers. Global companies will be able to localize work across markets and channels without maintaining enormous production teams.

But there are some serious implications.

Production revenue will come under pressure

Production has traditionally been a reliable source of agency revenue. It creates lots of deliverables, lots of hours and lots of predictable work. Automation can reduce those billable hours much faster than agencies replace them with technology fees, licensing models or performance-based compensation.

Agencies that keep charging clients for traditional production while quietly using automation to do the work will also create an obvious trust problem. Clients will eventually ask why they are paying the same amount for work that now takes a fraction of the time.

The entry-level creative ladder may break

Production roles have long been an important way into the creative industry. Junior designers learn brands, tools, workflows, craft and judgment by adapting and finishing the work of more experienced people. They may start by resizing an ad, but along the way they learn why the design works.

If those jobs disappear, agencies could weaken the talent pipeline that eventually produces senior designers, art directors and creative directors. The industry cannot remove the bottom rung of the ladder and assume people will somehow arrive at the top.

More content will not necessarily mean better content

Automation can produce a huge amount of technically correct but creatively forgettable work. When every advertiser can create hundreds of variations, volume stops being an advantage. The scarce capability becomes knowing which ideas deserve to be made in the first place.

Taste, originality and judgment become more valuable, not less. The real danger is not that machines will suddenly become great creative thinkers. It is that organizations will accept average work because it is fast, inexpensive and easy to measure.

Governance becomes part of the creative product

Automated production also creates new risks. Someone still needs to be accountable for inaccurate claims, inconsistent branding, licensing violations, cultural mistakes, inaccessible design, outdated product information and strange AI-generated imagery.

Systems will need to know what they are allowed to create, which source materials they can use and when a human needs to step in. Removing production designers without building a strong quality and governance layer would simply trade labor efficiency for brand risk.

More production will move to clients and platforms

Clients will also bring more of this work in-house. Tools such as WPP Open Pro, Adobe GenStudio, Meta's creative solutions and Google's campaign products are designed to put more creation, variation and activation directly into marketers' hands.

That does not mean clients will no longer need agencies. But agencies will need to prove that their value comes from the creative thinking, systems, intelligence and judgment surrounding production, not simply from access to people who can produce files.

The WPP and Meta integration is a useful preview of where this is going. Creative strategy, generation, media performance and optimization start to become parts of one continuous process. Once that happens, the traditional handoff from creative to production to media begins to look less like a workflow and more like a delay.

This is not really a software story

The biggest mistake agencies could make is treating this as another software-efficiency program. It is an operating-model change.

Agencies will need to retrain production designers before demand for their current work falls away. They will need commercial models that monetize platforms, governance, creative intelligence and business outcomes instead of hours.

They will need a new way to develop junior talent. They will also need clear standards defining which work can be automated, which work needs human review and which work should remain intentionally human.

The meaningful decline of the traditional production designer is likely to begin within the next two years and accelerate through the end of the decade. That does not have to mean the disappearance of production talent.

It could mean its elevation — from manually creating endless variations to designing, directing and governing the systems through which modern brands create at scale. But that evolution will only happen if agencies start preparing people for the next role before the current one begins to disappear.
