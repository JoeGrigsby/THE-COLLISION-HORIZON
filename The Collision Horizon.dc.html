▸ ABSTRACT

This is a scenario forecast, not a prediction. It traces two trajectories — the measured acceleration of frontier AI capability, and the structural adaptation of the marketing industry — and asks what happens where they intersect.

The timeline that follows is deliberately specific. Specificity is what makes a forecast falsifiable, and a falsifiable forecast is something a working group can argue with. Treat every date as a hypothesis and every claim as a position to be defended or revised.

And it spans the whole ecosystem, not one corner of it: the brands and marketers who commission the work, the agencies and holding companies who make it, the publishers and media owners who carry it, the platforms that increasingly generate and place it, and the consultancies moving into marketing from the other side. Each is repriced by the same force; on a different timeline.

The thesis is simple and uncomfortable: capability is compounding on a curve that the industry's operating model cannot match. The distance between the two, the collision horizon, is where the disruption of the next eight years is concentrated.

▸ THE TWO CURVES

AI capability advances roughly geometrically. Each model generation compresses the cost and time of cognitive work by an order of magnitude — and once systems begin improving themselves, that compounding accelerates again.

The industry adapts the way large organisations always have: in budget cycles, reorganisations, and multi-year transformation plans. Plot both on one axis, and they diverge. By the late 2020s, the gap is no longer a delay to manage; it is a structural condition to survive.

2026 // 0—12 MONTHS

THE SETUP

HIGH PRESSURE

The industry is in a period of consolidation. We are already seeing holding companies collapse legacy agency brands into integrated, more AI-enabled operating units; production work migrates onto shared content platforms; and identity and clean-room data layers are being stood up to replace the third-party signals that have disappeared. The logic is cost and coherence, and it is sound on its own terms.

Underneath it, the ground is already moving. The first genuinely autonomous agents begin automating coding, research, and adaptation work; the same entry-level labour the consolidation quietly assumed it could still draw on. Cheaper models flood the market within months, and a wave of lightweight AI tools puts agency-grade capability one subscription away from any client.

⚡ COLLISION POINT

Consolidation is rational. But it is optimising an operating model whose junior tier is being automated out from under it.

2027 // 12—24 MONTHS

THE THRESHOLD

CRITICAL

Networks target a return to organic growth, putting the new integrated model to its first real test. Unified creative groups must prove a value proposition clients cannot simply assemble themselves; media businesses are forced to demonstrate AI-era value beyond buying aggregation; and the global client lead replaces the agency P&L as the primary commercial unit.

Then the threshold is crossed. AI passes the superhuman-coder milestone, performing any coding task faster and cheaper than the best human engineers, and frontier labs run hundreds of thousands of agent copies at many times human speed. A general-capability system is announced publicly. Every efficiency argument the industry makes to a client is now also a capability the client can license directly.

Nowhere does this land faster than in production design. The rules-based craft of resizing, versioning, and localising assets, long a dependable column of agency fees, is the first to be absorbed as creative-automation systems connect to brand data, media performance, and publishing.

⚡ COLLISION POINT

The industry plans for a growth return precisely as capability crosses the line that makes its core service self-serve.

2028 // 18—30 MONTHS

THE ACCELERATION

CRITICAL

Marketing platforms mature into full agentic systems; plan, create, publish, and optimise in one continuous loop. The first serious acquisition targets AI infrastructure rather than agency capability. Production must either pivot upward into creative direction or accept commoditisation.

And the curve goes vertical. A superhuman AI researcher, qualitatively better than any human at advancing AI itself, pushes the rate of algorithmic progress toward a year of work every week. Alignment concerns spill into public hearings and international summits. The economy the industry is selling into is being restructured in real time.

⚡ COLLISION POINT

"Acceleration" assumes a stable environment to grow into. Instead it arrives into a market where the clients themselves are in crisis.

2029 // 2—3 YEARS

THE RECKONING

CRITICAL

Media businesses face an existential question: if AI optimises spend better than any human, what exactly is being sold? Pitch economics collapse as clients generate competitive strategic alternatives in hours. A first wave of senior strategists is hollowed out, and major brands build serious in-house AI capability — bypassing the agency layer for execution entirely.

2030 // 3—4 YEARS

THE INVERSION

EXISTENTIAL

The holding-company model is formally dead, replaced by integrated operating companies or not at all. The revenue mix inverts: service fees shrink and platform, data and software revenue must replace them. Firms either acquire AI infrastructure — a transformative, balance-sheet-defining bet — or face steady valuation compression.

The talent model inverts with it: fewer but far more senior practitioners, with AI handling all execution. Human value migrates decisively toward accountability, creativity and relationships — the things a model cannot underwrite.

⚡ COLLISION POINT

Survival depends on a transition that had to begin before 2028. Firms that didn't move from labour arbitrage to platform are being absorbed or wound down.

2031/32 // 4—6 YEARS

THE NEW EQUILIBRIUM

STABILISING

A new shape settles. Survivors are either smaller high-margin platform businesses or significantly smaller service businesses. New revenue models harden around outcome-based fees, data licensing, and AI-infrastructure access. Brand relationships go direct, as AI increasingly intermediates between brand and consumer, where agencies once stood.

The sector workforce is plausibly 40–60% smaller than its 2025 peak, and the remaining talent is fundamentally different in profile. Legacy agency brands are either leveraged into something new or quietly dissolved.

⚡ COLLISION POINT

The question is no longer whether the industry survives — it's what the industry is. Only brand, relationships and institutional knowledge are non-replicable.

2033 // END STATE

THE DESTINATION

RESOLVED

By 2033, each surviving firm is one of three things: a high-margin data and platform business, a much-diminished services firm, or no longer independent. The clients who stayed did so for judgment, accountability, and proprietary data; never for execution, which is now super cheap and instant.

Human creativity, cultural judgment and ethical accountability are the premium products. The winners own data, infrastructure or trust — not labour. The transformation plans of 2026 are a distant memory; what matters is what the industry became after them.

⚡ THE TAKEAWAY

The industry's fate in 2033 was written in decisions made between 2026 and 2029. The window to choose what kind of business to become is open now — and it closes fast.

▸ THE EVIDENCE BASE

What the research actually says

The trajectory above is a scenario — but it is anchored to the public research on AI and work. That research splits into four camps, and which camp a study belongs to largely determines how specific its timing is.

Per-period sources are linked under each year above. The estimates below are among the most-cited dated figures in the field.

EMPLOYER SURVEYS
WEF · Forrester · PwC

Ask employers what they plan to do by a fixed year. The most concrete dates and role-level numbers — and the least peer review.

ECONOMIC MODELLING
McKinsey · Goldman Sachs · Acemoglu

Model task exposure and adoption. Dated share-of-hours figures, but explicitly no per-occupation job-loss claims.

CAPABILITY EXTRAPOLATION
METR

Extrapolate when AI can complete tasks of a given length. Dated capability thresholds, not labour-market outcomes.

OBSERVED USAGE
Anthropic · Stanford · Brookings

Measure what is happening right now rather than predicting dates. The reality check on every forecast above.

⚠ READ THE FIGURES CAREFULLY

Specificity is inversely correlated with rigour: the most precise dated predictions come from non-peer-reviewed consultancy and think-tank reports, while the peer-reviewed studies deliberately refuse to predict timing. And "exposure" is not "automation" is not "job loss." Treat every number here as a signpost, not a guarantee.

▸ WHAT TO DO ABOUT IT

Four moves, while there is still pricing power

None of this is destiny. The forecast describes the pressure; these are the responses available to anyone in the industry who acts before the gap closes.

01

Move value up the stack

Relocate from execution to judgment, accountability and proprietary insight before 2028 — not after the market has already re-priced labour to zero.

02

Own data and identity

The only durable moat is owned, consented data infrastructure. Treat it as the core asset of the firm, not as one service line among many.

03

Re-price for outcomes

Abandon labour-hour economics. Shift to outcome-based fees, data licensing and platform access while the relationship still carries pricing power.

04

Rebuild the talent pyramid

The junior tier that fed the senior tier is gone. Design the smaller, AI-leveraged organisation deliberately — before attrition designs it for you.
